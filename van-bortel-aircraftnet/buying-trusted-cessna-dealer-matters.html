Why Buying From a Trusted Cessna Dealer Matters

📅 June 18, 2026
✎ Van Bortel Aircraft Team
⌚ 6 min read

The aircraft matters. So does who you buy it from — and for most owners, the dealer relationship outlasts the paperwork by years.

Shopping for a used Cessna can feel like a search for the lowest number on a listing page. But the price on a spec sheet doesn't tell you whether the logbooks are complete, whether the airframe was inspected by someone who actually understands Cessnas, or whether anyone will pick up the phone two years after the sale. That's the part a trusted dealer provides, and it's the part most buyers only think about after something goes wrong.

What "Trusted" Actually Means

Van Bortel Aircraft was founded in 1985 by Howard G. Van Bortel, who started the company with $1,000 and a card table in his sister's living room. Four decades later, the business he built is recognized as the world's most trusted Cessna dealer — not because of a slogan, but because of a Cessna Propjet Service Center designation earned in 1997, an FAA Authorized Repair Station certification held for more than 30 years, and a track record customers keep coming back to.

Trust in this business is built the slow way: through inspections that don't cut corners, through honest answers about an aircraft's history, and through a dealer who is still there for questions long after delivery. Howard's own principle — never compromise, and care for customers like no one else — has stayed the operating standard as the company grew.

We care about people — our customers, vendors, and employees. Doing the right thing by them is what we're all about.

The Cost of Skipping This Step

A dealer with a weak reputation has less at stake in getting your sale right. A dealer whose name is on decades of Cessna transactions has everything at stake. That difference shows up in how thoroughly an aircraft is inspected before it's offered for sale, how completely its maintenance history is documented, and how a dealer responds when a buyer has a question six months after closing.

It also shows up in financing. Van Bortel Aircraft established its own finance arm, Van Bortel Finance Corporation, in 2010, and within two years had helped finance more than $30,000,000 in Cessna aircraft. A dealer who also understands financing tends to structure the whole transaction — aircraft, inspection, and terms — around what actually works for the buyer.

How to Evaluate a Dealer Before You Evaluate an Aircraft

Ask how long the dealer has been in business, and ask specifically about service history — not just sales volume. A dealer with an in-house, FAA-authorized repair station has a very different relationship with the aircraft it sells than one that simply lists inventory. Ask, too, about what happens after the sale: does the relationship end at delivery, or does the dealer stay reachable for maintenance questions, parts, and future upgrades?

Finally, look at longevity in the specific niche you're shopping. A dealer that specializes in Independence-built Cessnas and has sold them for decades will simply know more about that airframe's quirks, values, and maintenance patterns than a generalist ever could — and that knowledge is worth more than a slightly lower asking price.

Conclusion

The aircraft you buy will only ever be as good as the dealer standing behind it. Before you fall for a listing, spend a little time evaluating the business on the other end of the phone.
